Family Flat Rent in Dhaka: A Practical Checklist Before You Sign (2026)

Renting a flat for your family is a bigger decision than renting as a single person. You're choosing schools nearby, a safe building, enough space, good neighbors, and a place you won't want to leave in a year. Get it wrong and moving a whole family again is exhausting and expensive.

The good news: families have the easiest time in Dhaka's rental market. Most landlords prefer family tenants, so doors that stay shut to bachelors open readily for you. That means your job isn't to convince landlords — it's to choose well and check carefully before you commit. This checklist walks you through exactly that.

For the full picture of how the search works, start with our main guide on how to find a flat to rent in Dhaka.

Before you start: get your priorities straight

Families have more boxes to tick than single renters, so decide what matters most before you look:

  • Budget — your comfortable monthly rent, plus one month's advance (the norm in Bangladesh).
  • Location — close to work, schools, and family if possible.
  • Size — bedrooms, and whether you need space to grow.
  • Must-haves vs nice-to-haves — lift, parking, sunlight, water supply, security.

Knowing this upfront stops you falling for a beautiful flat that's wrong on the things that actually matter day to day.

Choosing the right area for a family

Different neighborhoods suit families differently. The three most popular affordable options each have a distinct character:

  • Uttara — planned, spacious, quieter, with schools and malls; great for families who don't mind being farther from the center. (More in our area comparison.)
  • Mohammadpur — central and convenient, with good access to schools and hospitals.
  • Mirpur — affordable and well-connected, with everything nearby, though denser and busier.

Match the area to your family's life — school runs, commutes, and the kind of environment you want your children growing up in.

The flat inspection checklist

Never rent a family flat without seeing it in person. When you visit, check:

Water and utilities - Is the water supply reliable, with good pressure? Ask neighbors, not just the landlord. - Electricity — any frequent load-shedding in the building? Is there a generator? - Gas supply — piped gas, or will you use cylinders?

The flat itself - Natural light and ventilation in the main rooms. - Condition of bathrooms, kitchen, walls, and ceilings (look for damp or leaks). - Enough bedrooms and usable space for your family. - Mobile network and internet availability.

The building and surroundings - Lift (if upper floor) and whether it's well-maintained. - Security — guard, gate, overall safety of the building. - Parking, if you have a vehicle. - Noise levels and the general feel of the neighbors.

The neighborhood - Distance to schools, hospitals, markets, and your workplace. - Safety of the area, especially for children. - Access to public transport.

What to ask the landlord

Before you commit, get clear answers on:

  • Exact monthly rent and what (if anything) it includes.
  • Advance amount — usually one month — and whether it's refundable.
  • Any annual rent increase — how much, and how often.
  • Notice period for either side to end the tenancy.
  • Building rules that affect family life (guests, children, common areas).
  • Who handles repairs and maintenance issues.

Get it in writing before you pay

This is the step families most often skip — and most often regret. Insist on a written rental agreement covering:

  • Monthly rent and advance amount.
  • Refund terms for the advance.
  • Rent increase terms.
  • Notice period.
  • The flat's condition at move-in (note any existing damage so you're not blamed later).

Both sides sign, both keep a copy. If a landlord won't put things in writing, treat it as a warning about how any future dispute will go.

The family renter's quick checklist

  1. Set budget, area, and must-haves before you start.
  2. Shortlist flats in family-friendly areas that fit your needs.
  3. Visit in person — inspect water, power, gas, light, security, and neighbors.
  4. Confirm rent, one-month advance, refund terms, and any rent increase.
  5. Deal directly with the owner; never pay anyone just to view a flat.
  6. Sign a written agreement and document the flat's condition.
  7. Keep your copy of everything.
