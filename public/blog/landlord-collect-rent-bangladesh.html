Collecting Rent in Bangladesh: How Landlords Can Avoid Unpaid Rent and bKash Fees (2026)

Most rental guides are written for tenants. This one is for the other side — the landlord. If you own a flat in Bangladesh, you already know the quiet headaches that come with it: the tenant who disappears owing two months' rent, the awkwardness of knocking on a door every month to ask for money, and the fees that nibble away at every bKash transaction.

None of these are small problems. Over a year, an unpaid tenant or a month of chasing rent costs you real money and real peace of mind. Here's how landlords in Bangladesh can protect themselves, collect rent reliably, and stop losing money to avoidable fees.

For the tenant's side of the market, see our main guide on how to find a flat to rent in Dhaka.

Problem one: the tenant who escapes without paying

Every landlord's worst case is the tenant who stops paying and then vanishes — sometimes leaving unpaid utility bills behind too. Once they're gone, recovering the money is slow, expensive, and often impossible. Prevention is your only real protection.

Screen tenants before they move in

The best defence happens before you hand over the keys. A few minutes of checking saves you months of loss:

  • Verify identity properly. Take an NID copy and confirm it matches the person. Note their permanent address, not just the present one.
  • Ask for a workplace or income reference. Knowing where someone works — and being able to confirm it — is a strong deterrent. A tenant whose employer you can contact is far less likely to skip.
  • Get a previous-landlord reference where possible. The person who rented to them last knows whether they paid on time.
  • Collect emergency contacts. A family member's number and address gives you a way to follow up if the tenant disappears.

Use the advance as a buffer

The standard one month's advance in Bangladesh exists partly for this reason — it's your cushion against a final unpaid month or damage. Make sure the advance amount and what it covers are written clearly in your agreement, so there's no dispute when the tenancy ends.

Always use a written agreement

A signed rental agreement is your strongest tool. It should state the rent, the advance, the payment date each month, the notice period, and the consequences of non-payment. A tenant who has signed a clear document behaves differently from one operating on a vague verbal understanding. If a dispute ever reaches a point of mediation or legal action, the written agreement is what protects you.

Problem two: chasing rent in person every month

Even tenants who pay can be exhausting to collect from when it means knocking on the door, waiting for "next week," and having the same conversation every month. It strains the relationship and wastes your time.

The fix is to move rent collection out of cash and into a fixed, agreed method from day one.

  • Set a clear payment date in the agreement — say, the 5th of every month — so there's never ambiguity about when rent is due.
  • Agree the payment channel upfront, in writing, so the tenant knows exactly how to pay without being asked.
  • Keep a simple record of each month's payment. A shared written log or message thread removes "I already paid" disputes.

The goal is to make paying rent automatic and expected, not something you have to request each time.

Problem three: bank transfers are underused — and bKash fees add up

In many countries, rent simply lands in the landlord's bank account each month. In Bangladesh, that habit hasn't fully taken hold — many tenants still prefer cash or mobile money, and not everyone is comfortable with bank transfers.

Mobile money, especially bKash, has filled the gap because it's everywhere and easy. But it comes with a cost that landlords feel on every transaction: fees on both sending and cashing out.

What bKash actually costs in 2026

It helps to know the real numbers so you can choose the cheapest path:

  • Cash out at a standard agent: about Tk 18.50 per Tk 1,000 (1.85%). On a Tk 20,000 rent, that's roughly Tk 370 gone every month just to withdraw.
  • Cash out via Priyo Agent or partner ATM: about Tk 14.90 per Tk 1,000 (1.49%) — cheaper, but capped at a monthly limit.
  • Send money between personal accounts is free up to a limit via the Priyo Number feature, but cashing out is where the cost lands.

Over a year, agent cash-out fees on a single flat's rent can quietly add up to thousands of taka. For a landlord with several flats, it's a meaningful leak.

How landlords can cut the fees

  • Move money bKash-to-bank instead of cashing out at an agent. Bank transfer routes are significantly cheaper than standard agent cash out — as of 2026, transferring from bKash to a bank account through the regulated NPSB system is capped at 0.85% (Tk 8.50 per Tk 1,000), roughly half the standard cash-out cost. If you're going to move the money to your bank anyway, this is far cheaper than cashing out as physical notes.
  • Use Priyo Agent cash out for the portion you do need as cash, to get the lower 1.49% rate up to the monthly cap.
  • Encourage direct bank transfer for rent. The cheapest fee is the one you never pay. If you can get tenants comfortable paying rent straight into your bank account, you avoid mobile-money cash-out fees entirely. Framing it as easier for both sides — no cash to carry, an automatic record of payment — helps tenants come around.
  • Build the method into the agreement. Whatever you choose, write it in so it's the default from month one.

A landlord's rent-collection checklist

  1. Screen every tenant: verify NID, get workplace and previous-landlord references, and collect emergency contacts.
  2. Take the standard one month's advance as a buffer, clearly documented.
  3. Sign a written agreement with the rent amount, payment date, and notice period.
  4. Agree the payment channel and date upfront so you never have to chase.
  5. Prefer direct bank transfer where possible to avoid mobile-money fees.
  6. If using bKash, move money bKash-to-bank rather than cashing out at an agent to roughly halve the fee.
  7. Keep a written record of every payment.

The bigger picture: renting works better when it's organised

Most of a landlord's headaches — unpaid rent, monthly chasing, fee leakage — trace back to the same root: renting in Bangladesh is still done informally, with little structure around tenant verification, agreements, or payments. The landlords who put simple systems in place — proper screening, a written agreement, an agreed digital payment method — lose far less to bad tenants and wasted time.

Final word

Owning a rental flat in Bangladesh doesn't have to mean unpaid months and monthly door-knocking. Screen tenants properly, put everything in a written agreement, agree a reliable payment method upfront, and choose the cheapest way to move your money. Do that, and you keep more of your rent — and a lot more of your peace of mind.
